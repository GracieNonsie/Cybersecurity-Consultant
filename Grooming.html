What is Grooming ?




The term "sexual grooming" describes acts or behaviors intended to decrease a minor's inhibitions with the intention of sexual abuse by forming an emotional bond with them and, in some cases, their family.In different contexts, such as online, in person, or through other forms of communication, it can happen .Mental health problems in children who are groomed may include "suicidal thoughts, anxiety, depression, and post-traumatic stress disorder."




History And Recognition :


Origin

Before the term "grooming" was associated with grooming a child for sexual abuse, it had come to have a meaning of mentorship, coaching, or preparing someone for leadership.
Between 1975 and 1985, child sexual abuse cases involving non-strangers that affected children outside of their families were brought to the attention of American law enforcement. In the past, law enforcement had concentrated on individuals who posed a "stranger danger" and threatened violence in order to get their victims to comply.
The children in these recently identified episodes of sexual abuse were coerced by a "combination of attention, affection, kindness, gifts, alcohol, drugs, money, and privileges."Although there have been instances when the pattern has been identified previously, it was in this decade that the FBI became aware of the pattern and criminal investigations started to receive considerable attention in the US. Recognition

Spyware often installs itself using Internet Explorer. Due to its widespread popularity and past security problems, it is frequently targeted. Because of its close ties to the Windows environment, it is vulnerable to attacks within the Windows OS. Additionally, malware attaches itself to Internet Explorer through Browser Helper Objects, which alter the behavior of the browser.

Legal Issues

Computer crime laws, such as the U.S. Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, the U.K. Computer Misuse Act, and comparable laws in other nations, prohibit unauthorized access to computers. On the surface, it would seem that spreading spyware would be illegal because most computer owners who have spyware on them say they never gave permission for it to be installed. Law enforcement has frequently gone against those who create viruses and other types of malware.

Criminal Law

Though some have been sued, not many spyware makers have been convicted; instead, many of them run openly as completely legal enterprises. The makers of spyware contend that, in contrast to what users say, consumers actually approve installations. End-user license agreements (EULAs), which are written in legalese, may contain descriptions of spyware that is included with shareware apps.

Administrative sanctions

The US Federal Trade Commission has sued Internet marketing organizations under the "unfairness doctrine" to make them stop infecting consumers' PCs with spyware.
In one case, that against Seismic Entertainment Productions, the FTC accused the defendants of developing a program that seized control of PCs nationwide, infected them with spyware and other malicious software.



Netherlands OPTA

n administrative fine, the first of its kind in Europe, has been issued by the Independent Authority of Posts and Telecommunications (OPTA) from the Netherlands. It applied fines in total value of Euro 1,000,000 for infecting 22 million computers. The spyware concerned is called DollarRevenue.

Civil Law

Former New York State Attorney General and former Governor of New York Eliot Spitzer has pursued spyware companies for fraudulent installation of software.
In a suit brought in 2005 by Spitzer, the California firm Intermix Media, Inc. ended up settling, by agreeing to pay US$7.5 million and to stop distributing spyware.

Web Hijacking

The hijacking of Web advertisements has also led to litigation. In June 2002, a number of large Web publishers sued Claria for replacing advertisements, but settled out of court.

Libel Suits by Software Developers

Litigation has gone both ways. Since "spyware" has become a common pejorative, some makers have filed libel and defamation actions when their products have been so described.

WebcamGate

In the 2010 WebcamGate case, plaintiffs charged two suburban Philadelphia high schools secretly spied on students by surreptitiously and remotely activating webcams embedded in school-issued laptops the students were using at home, and therefore infringed on their privacy rights. The school loaded each student's computer with LANrev's remote activation tracking software. This included the now-discontinued "TheftTrack".
While TheftTrack was not enabled by default on the software, the program allowed the school district to elect to activate it, and to choose which of the TheftTrack surveillance options the school wanted to enable.
